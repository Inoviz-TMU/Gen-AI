1Tokenization

Before a machine can think about words, it needs to cut them up.

A neural network only understands numbers. So the very first job — before any "intelligence" happens — is turning text into a sequence of discrete pieces. This piece is called a token.

Imagine you can't read English, but I hand you a long string of beads, each bead with a number painted on it. You don't know what the numbers mean yet — but if I give you enough strings of beads, and tell you which strings "go together" in the real world, you can start learning patterns. That's all a token is: a bead with a number on it. The model never sees letters. It sees beads.

— the core idea, before any math

Three ways to cut a sentence

Before we settle on the real answer, let's see why the obvious approaches fail.

ApproachHow it splitsThe problem
Character-level t-h-e- -c-a-t- -s-a-t... Sequences become huge. "Understanding" takes 13 steps just to spell one word — the model has to work hard before it even gets to meaning.
Word-level The / cat / sat / on / the / mat Vocabulary explodes — every language has hundreds of thousands of words, plus typos, slang, and new words break it completely. "unbelievably" and "unbelievable" become two unrelated entries.
Subword (BPE) The / cat / sat / on / the / mat (common words stay whole) This is what production models actually use. Rare or unseen words get broken into familiar pieces instead of failing outright.

Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE)

Here's the entire algorithm in one sentence: start with individual characters, then repeatedly merge whichever pair appears most often, until you have a vocabulary of a useful size. That's it. No grammar rules. No dictionary. Just "what pairs show up together a lot, over and over, across a giant pile of text."

BPE merging "lower" + "lowest" — shared pieces get merged into reusable chunks
Why this matters: this is exactly why GPT-style models can handle a word they've never seen, a typo, or even code — because the word just gets sliced into smaller familiar pieces instead of crashing into an "unknown word" wall.

2Embeddings

A token ID is just an address. An embedding is the meaning that lives there.

Token IDs (147, 2391, 88...) carry zero meaning — token 5000 isn't "more" than token 10 in any sense. We need to convert each ID into something that captures what the word means, and where it sits relative to every other word in meaning-space.

Think of it like this: imagine every word in the language gets a house on an enormous map. Words that mean similar things get houses near each other. "King" and "Queen" are neighbors. "Happy" and "Joyful" are practically sharing a wall. "Bank" — the money kind — lives near "money" and "loan." But here's the twist: that same word "bank" also has a *second* house, near "river" and "shore," for when it means riverbank. The embedding is just the coordinates of that house. Except instead of 2 coordinates like a map (x, y), we use hundreds or thousands of coordinates — because meaning has way more than two dimensions to it.

— why we need vectors, not just numbers

From token ID → dense vector

Each token ID indexes into a giant lookup table — that row of numbers IS the word's meaning

Here's the part that feels like magic but isn't: nobody hand-writes these numbers. They start out random, and then get nudged, bit by bit, during training — every time the model is wrong about predicting a word, the nudge adjusts these vectors a tiny bit so similar words drift toward each other in this space.

The famous demonstration: vector arithmetic

If embeddings really capture meaning as geometry, then meaning should behave like geometry. And it does — astonishingly:

king − man + woman ≈ queen — direction in vector space encodes relationship, not just identity

What "close together" actually means

We measure closeness with cosine similarity — basically, "do these two vectors point in roughly the same direction?" Same direction = similar meaning, regardless of magnitude.

How many dimensions?

Real models use anywhere from 256 to 4096+ dimensions per word. You can't visualize that — so every 2D plot you see of embeddings is a heavy compression (via PCA or t-SNE) of a much higher-dimensional reality.

Important nuance for later: this embedding is the word's meaning in isolation — before it has read the rest of the sentence. "Bank" gets the exact same starting vector whether the sentence is about rivers or money. Fixing that is literally what the Transformer block does next. Hold that thought.

There's a subtlety worth pausing on. The embedding table we just described lives inside a language model and exists to predict the next token. Key idea: Matryoshka embeddings

Newer models let you generate one big vector, then truncate it shorter for cheaper storage — quality degrades gracefully instead of falling off a cliff. Named after the nesting dolls.

Worth remembering

An embedding model's whole job ends at the vector. It doesn't generate text. The Transformer is what takes embeddings and actually reasons over them — which is exactly where we're headed next.

4The Transformer

Now the real machine: how does a model read a whole sentence at once?

Before 2017, models like RNNs read sentences one word at a time, left to right, like reading with your finger covering everything except the current word. That's slow, and it forgets things that happened many words ago. The Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017, "Attention Is All You Need") threw that out entirely: let every word look at every other word, all at once.

Picture a classroom discussion instead of kids passing a note one at a time down a row. In the old way (RNN), word 1 whispers to word 2, who whispers to word 3, and by word 20 the original message has gotten garbled — like a long game of telephone. In the Transformer's way, every single word stands up and says, "here's what I think is relevant to me," and listens to every other word simultaneously, weighing some more heavily than others. No whispering down a chain. No garbling. Just one big, parallel conversation.

— why "attention" beat recurrence

The big picture: encoder and decoder stacks

This is the architecture from the original paper — and the one Jay Alamar's "Illustrated Transformer" made famous for building intuition. Don't worry about memorizing every box; focus on the shape of the idea.

The full encoder–decoder Transformer — each block is repeated N times (commonly N=6)

Encoder's job

Read the entire input sentence and build a rich representation of it — where every word's vector now also contains context from every other word.

Decoder's job

Generate the output one token at a time, looking back both at what it has generated so far and at the encoder's representation of the input.

Connecting back to session one: this is the same encoder/decoder language you already covered — translation models use both halves. Modern LLMs like GPT are decoder-only — they dropped the encoder and just kept stacking decoder blocks, trained to predict the next token. BERT-style models went the other way: encoder-only, built for understanding rather than generating.

Zooming into one block

Every one of those repeated boxes contains the same two ingredients. Let's name them now — we'll unpack each one in the next two sections.

Inside a single encoder block — self-attention, then a small feed-forward network, with residual connections wrapping both
5Self-Attention

The heart of it all: Query, Key, and Value.

This is the one mechanism that makes everything else possible. Let's build the intuition completely before touching a single piece of matrix math.

You walk into a library looking for a book about "cats." That's your Query — what you're searching for. Every book on every shelf has a label on its spine summarizing what it's about — that's the book's Key. You scan all the spines and compare your query against each key — "how well does this book's label match what I'm looking for?" Books about cats score high. Books about tax law score low. Once you know how well each book matches, you don't just grab the best match and ignore the rest — you grab a blend, weighted by how relevant each book was. The actual content inside each book — what you walk away having read — is the Value. Self-attention is exactly this, done by every single word, searching across every other word in the sentence, simultaneously.

— Query, Key, Value, the library analogy

Why "self"? Why does "it" need attention at all?

Take this classic example sentence:

The word "it" must figure out, from context, what it's actually referring to — that's what attention weights solve

A plain embedding can't solve this — "it" has the exact same starting vector no matter what sentence it's in. Self-attention is the mechanism that lets "it" look around the sentence and pull in context, so its vector afterward actually encodes "the cat" — not just the dictionary definition of the pronoun "it."

Now let's trace it on our own sentence

Back to "The cat sat on the mat." Watch how much attention the word "sat" pays to every other word in the sentence.

Darker connection = higher attention weight. "Sat" attends most strongly to "cat" (who's doing the sitting) and "mat" (where the sitting happens)

The actual mechanics (now that the intuition is solid)

Each word's embedding is multiplied by three different learned matrices to produce its Query, Key, and Value vectors

The four-step recipe

  • 1. Turn every word into a Query, a Key, and a Value (three separate learned projections)
  • 2. Score: compare every Query against every Key (dot product) — "how relevant is this word to me?"
  • 3. Normalize those scores into weights that sum to 1 (softmax) — turns raw scores into a proper "blend ratio"
  • 4. Blend: mix all the Values together using those weights — that blend becomes the word's new, context-aware vector

Why "multi-head" attention?

One attention pass might learn to track grammar (who's the subject?). Another might track reference (what does "it" point to?). Another might track tone. So Transformers run several attention "heads" in parallel — each free to specialize — then combine their outputs. It's the same sentence read several different ways at once, simultaneously, by different specialists.

6Positional Encoding

There's one thing attention forgets entirely: order.

Here's a sharp problem with what we just built. Self-attention compares every word to every other word — but nothing in that comparison cares where a word sits in the sentence. Mathematically, attention treats a sentence like a bag of words thrown in the air.

Without position information, attention literally cannot tell these two sentences apart — same words, same set, different meaning

Imagine handing someone a shuffled deck of flashcards, one word per card, and saying "tell me what sentence this is." They can group the cards by similarity all day long — but without numbering the cards, they can never reconstruct the order, and order is half of what a sentence means. So before we feed words into attention, we stamp a small, unique number-pattern onto every word's vector based purely on its position: 1st word, 2nd word, 3rd word... That stamp rides along with the word's meaning through the entire network.

— why position has to be injected manually

The clever trick: sine and cosine waves

The original Transformer paper's solution is genuinely elegant: for each position in the sentence, generate a unique pattern using sine and cosine waves of different frequencies, then simply add that pattern directly on top of the word's embedding vector.

Each position gets its own unique wave-fingerprint — low frequencies capture "big picture" position, high frequencies capture fine-grained position

Why waves, specifically?

Waves repeat smoothly, so the model can learn relative relationships ("the word two positions after me") without needing a separate lookup table for every possible sentence length. It generalizes to sentences longer than anything seen in training.

Modern variants

Many current LLMs (like LLaMA) use RoPE (Rotary Position Embeddings) instead — a more refined version of the same core idea: rotate vectors based on position rather than just adding a fixed pattern. The intuition is identical; only the implementation is fancier.

2 → 3: Add Position

A unique positional wave-pattern is added to each vector, so "first word" and "fourth word" are now distinguishable, even if they're the same word.

3 → 4: Self-Attention

Every word's vector gets updated by blending in context from every other word, weighted by relevance — meaning now flows across the whole sentence.

The one idea to leave the room with: a Transformer doesn't "understand" English the way we do. It's a very disciplined machine for turning words into vectors, letting those vectors negotiate meaning with each other through attention, and repeating that negotiation many times over until the final vectors are rich enough to predict — astonishingly well — what word should come next.
